# Four hours, not forty

Let me tell you the real reason networking lapses - because it isn't the one you think.

It isn't laziness, and it isn't that you're bad at it. It's that networking done properly is *mentally expensive*. Think about what a single good touch actually costs you: open their profile, remember who they are and what you last spoke about, work out the right thing to say, say it well, then keep track of where every one of these little threads has got to. None of that is hard, exactly. But it's *load* - and mental load is the thing you run out of long before you run out of hours. It's why the good intention dies at 9pm, and why the whole effort quietly lapses in your busy weeks, which are precisely the weeks it mattered.

Notice what that means. The barrier was never the quality of your messages. Most people privately think their problem is that they're not clever or charming enough on the page. They're wrong. The barrier is that doing the thoughtful thing, per person, again and again, costs more mental energy than a busy professional has spare. The bottleneck isn't talent. It's friction.

And friction is exactly what's now removable.

Not the judgment - that stays yours. Not the relationships - those are the whole point, and they're yours too. What lifts off is the draggy, expensive part around them. You jump in of a morning and say, in plain words, "prep me for today's meetings," and you get briefed from every conversation you've ever had with those people - like having an assistant who happened to sit in all your meetings and forgot none of it. "Draft the follow-up," and there it is, because the thing knows your calendar, your past calls and your emails, not just a name to drop into a template.

Here's the part I want to be careful about, though, because it's where people misread the whole thing. The tech is not the point. It is not a differentiator, and it's not meant to impress you. Tech and AI are simply *expected* now - the way a phone is. Fairly soon nobody will say "an AI business," they'll just say "a business," and the AI will be humming away in the background where it belongs. The reason it matters here isn't that it's clever. It's that it takes the one thing that used to make network building unaffordable - the mental load - and drops it close to zero.

Which is where the title comes from. Work that used to cost you the equivalent of forty hours of dread and effort - or, more honestly, work that simply never got done because the load was too high - now costs something like four. And because it costs almost nothing, you actually do it. Consistently. Which, as we've seen, was the entire game all along. The quiet gift here isn't speed. It's that the floor comes up: the tired-you at the end of a long day still sends something genuinely good, because the expensive part isn't resting on your willpower anymore.

Now - if you've been reading all this thinking *"hang on, if it's just tech doing the heavy lifting, couldn't I simply build that myself?"* - that's a fair thought, and a common one. It deserves a proper answer.

That's next.
