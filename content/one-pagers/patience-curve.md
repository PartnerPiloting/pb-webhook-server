# The patience curve

Most people who quit networking quit at about month three.

They did everything right. Chose well, reached out, had some good conversations, even a promising call or two. And then they look up at the three-month mark, tally what it's actually produced in hard revenue, find it thin - and conclude, reasonably enough, that networking doesn't work. So they stop. Right there.

Here's what makes that so painful: they quit in the flat part of the curve. The part right before it stops being flat.

Think about anything that compounds - savings, fitness, a language, a garden. They all share the same shape at the start: you put in real, consistent effort, and for a while almost nothing visible happens. The needle barely moves. Every instinct tells you it isn't working. And then - not gradually, but at some tipping point you can never quite predict in advance - it bends. The savings throw off real interest. The introductions start arriving on their own. What looked flat was never flat. It was loading.

Networking is exactly this shape, and almost nobody is told so up front. It **builds, it doesn't spike.** There's no viral moment, no single week where it all lands at once. What's happening in those quiet early months is real even when the bank balance says otherwise: you're accumulating chosen relationships, and those relationships are slowly coming to know your work well enough to put their name behind it. That takes the time it takes. You can't hurry someone into trusting you enough to recommend you.

Which means the whole game - genuinely, the whole thing - comes down to two unglamorous words: consistency and patience.

Not intensity. This isn't a heroic burst - forty coffees in a fortnight and then nothing. It's the opposite. A steady, unremarkable presence, month after month, well past the point where a normal person would have given up. The people who win at this aren't the most charming or the best connected. They're the ones who were still going in month six, and month twelve, when the curve finally did what curves do.

I'll be honest with you, because this whole series has tried to be: I can't make it fast. Nobody can. Anyone promising you a full pipeline by next month is selling you the exact thing this piece is warning you about.

What I *can* tell you is that it's real - and that the reason most people never see it isn't that it doesn't work. It's that they stopped in the flat part. The single biggest thing standing between you and a network that feeds itself isn't talent, or luck, or even the right method. It's simply not quitting before the curve bends.

There is, mind you, an obvious temptation lurking in all this. If building a network takes months of patience - couldn't you just *buy* one? Skip the wait, pay for an instant, ready-made network, today? People will happily sell you exactly that.

Whether it works is next.
<!-- /ENDING:PROSPECT -->

<!-- ENDING:CLIENT -->
And here's the part that's genuinely new. For most of the history of networking, the curve was almost impossible to ride - not because people lacked patience, but because staying consistent by hand, month after month, was simply too much work, so it quietly lapsed in exactly the flat stretch where quitting is fatal. That's what's changed. When the effort of doing it properly drops to almost nothing, the one thing the curve asks of you - steady, unremarkable consistency - stops being a feat of discipline and becomes something you can actually sustain.

So when month three feels flat, don't read it as failure. Read it as loading. Keep the rhythm - it's the easy part now - and let the curve do what curves do.
