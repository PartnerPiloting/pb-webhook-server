# Building nodes

Step back for a moment and look at the whole thing.

Everything in this series has been one relationship at a time. Choose someone worth knowing. Reach out like a human. Thank them properly. Meet, and be curious. Give them a reason to come back. Patient, deliberate, one person after another. It works - but if that were all it ever was, it would still be *you*, doing it, forever.

This is where that changes.

Remember the quiet line from the last piece - the one you cross without noticing. You start as a *networker*: someone for whom networking is an activity, a thing you do. Keep at it with the right people and you become a *leader* - not in any grand sense, just someone others start to learn this from, who shows the good ones how to build networks of their own. And then the third stage, the one this whole series has been walking toward: you become a *builder*.

You build a node.

A node is small and deliberate - a handful of the right people, chosen for attitude, all quietly looking out for one another. On its own, that is already worth more than a contact list of five hundred. But watch what happens when a couple of the people in your node go and build nodes of their own, the way you showed them. And when you start being introduced to *other* builders - people running their own nodes, same attitude, same way of working - who you'd never have found alone.

Now the shape of the thing has changed entirely. You are no longer the hub of a wheel you personally have to keep spinning. You are one node in a web of them. And the introductions stop coming only from you, or only from the people you reached out to. They begin arriving from two and three steps away - from people you have never met, sending work your way, because the person who vouched for you was vouched for by someone who trusts you. Recommendations flow toward you down chains you didn't build and can't even see. **That** is a network that grows without you.

And here is the thing that makes you the person all of this happens around. Anyone can want a bigger network. Anyone can want a clever system for finding more people. What almost nobody does - and what quietly sets a builder apart - is go looking not for people to add to their own network, but for people who want to build a network of their own. That is the rare instinct. That, more than anything, is what you bring to the table.

Is it work? Of course it is. Becoming a builder, and then helping others become builders, is more than firing off connection requests and hoping. But look at what the work buys. Cast your mind back to where we started - the feast-and-famine see-saw, the low-grade dread of *where is the next piece of work coming from*. This is where that ends. Not because you finally found the discipline to network harder, but because you built something that feeds itself: a web of good people, all looking out for each other, sending work down the line long after you have stopped pushing.

Which leaves one last question - a small, human one. When you are the person at the centre of all this, what does it actually feel like, day to day? It comes down to a single sentence you will catch yourself saying more and more often. That's next.
