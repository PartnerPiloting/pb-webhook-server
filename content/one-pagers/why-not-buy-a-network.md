# Why not just buy a network?

If building a network takes months of patience, here's the obvious question - the one a sharp person asks straight away. Why build one at all? Why not just *buy* one?

Because you can. There's a whole category of paid networks that will sell you exactly this: pay a monthly fee - often a serious one, a thousand a month is not unusual - and you're handed a vetted, ready-made circle of professionals. You made the cut. Instant network, warm connections, no waiting. After the last piece, that sounds like the cheat code, doesn't it? Skip the flat part of the curve. Just pay.

I want to take the idea seriously, because it isn't a scam. Those networks are real, some are well run, and the instinct behind them - that a curated room beats a random one - is exactly right. It's the same instinct this whole series is built on.

But look closely at what you're actually buying, and three cracks appear.

The first: it's the breakfast club again, in a nicer suit. You still didn't choose who's in the room. Someone else did. You've upgraded from "whoever paid for the breakfast" to "whoever paid the bigger membership," but the fundamental thing hasn't changed - it's *their* room, assembled to *their* criteria, and you're a guest in it. The moment you stop paying, it's gone. You never owned a thing.

The second is subtler, and it's about the referrals themselves. A network that has promised you warm introductions now has to *produce* them - that's the deal, that's what your money bought. So it does what it must: it refers. You become "the accountant" they send people to - and so do the other four accountants who paid the same fee. The exclusivity you thought you were buying gets quietly diluted, because the business model depends on everyone getting enough referrals to renew. It's the quota problem wearing a premium badge.

The third is the one that makes people wince once they see it: you're paying, every month, to do work whose real value accrues to *them*. You build the relationships. They own the platform. Stop the subscription and you walk away with nothing but a lighter wallet.

So here's the version that actually works - and it costs a membership fee of exactly zero.

You build one small, deliberate node. A handful of the right people, chosen by you, on attitude - one really good accountant, one really good lawyer, one of each thing that matters. Small and curated, and *yours*. Then watch what happens when you meet another person who's built a node the same way. You get talking, and he mentions his accountant. "Ah - I'm covered there, I've got a great one," you say. "But who else have you got?" And he runs through his little curated list, and somewhere in it is exactly the person you've been missing. "I don't have a good one of those yet." "Perfect - let me introduce you."

Look at what just happened. Two small nodes *docked*. In one conversation you each opened your curated circle to the other - the instant-network benefit the paid platform charges a thousand a month for - except nobody paid, nothing was forced, and the exclusivity held, because the seat in your node is taken. You're *his* accountant now, not one of five. And the vetting the platform sells? Already done - by the attitude filter you both used to build your nodes in the first place.

That's the whole difference between renting and owning. A bought network is someone else's room you pay to stand in. A built one is an asset you keep - and, it turns out, one that grows every time it meets another like it.

Which raises a rather thrilling question: what happens when it isn't just two nodes, but many?

That's where we've been heading all along.
