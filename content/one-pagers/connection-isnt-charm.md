# Connection isn't charm

For a lot of people - and technical people most of all - the scariest part of meeting someone new isn't the substance. It's the first two minutes.

The bit before you get down to it - a call, a coffee, a first conversation of any kind. The "how's your weekend, how's the weather" bit. If you're someone who'd rather just talk about the actual work, this stretch can feel like a tax you don't know how to pay - a performance of easy chit-chat that some people seem born for and you, apparently, are not. So you rush it, or skip it, and the thing never quite warms up.

Here's the secret nobody tells you: connection isn't charm. It isn't banter, it isn't quick wit, and it is absolutely not a personality you're either born with or you're not. It's a procedure - two small moves - and anyone can run it, including you.

Here they are.

**Move one: notice one true, specific thing about them - and say it.**

Not "nice weather." Specific. Something actually in front of you. The city they're in. The bookshelf or the plants behind them. A post they wrote. Where they used to work. You're not manufacturing a compliment - you're noticing something real and mentioning it out loud. *"I was just reading your post from a few months back - you clearly threw everything at that."* *"I love the plants behind you - that's not a virtual background, is it?"* *"You're in Sydney - that's where I started out."*

That's the whole move. And the other person's reaction isn't "what a charmer." It's the quiet, disarming feeling of being *seen* - and people relax the instant they feel seen. It's the same thing a good thank-you message does, just out loud: specific attention, freely given.

**Move two: offer one true, specific thing back.**

Connection is a trade, not an interview. So don't just ask "how was your weekend?" and nod along - answer it yourself, with something real. *"Mine was good - I've just taken up pickleball, no idea what I'm doing."* *"Sorry, I'm a bit scattered - the power just went out at my daughter's place, they're putting in a pool out the back."* A small, human, slightly imperfect detail of your own. It says: I'm a person here too, not a script. And it quietly gives them permission to be one back.

Now notice what neither move requires. Not speed. Not wit. Not the ability to think on your feet. In fact you can do the noticing *before the call even starts* - thirty seconds on their profile, a glance at where they're based, and you walk in already holding your one true thing. That's the part that should set a "hopeless at small talk" person free: this isn't improv. It's preparation. And preparation is something technical people happen to be excellent at.

And here's why "true and specific" carries all the weight: generic is actually worse than nothing. "How's the weather?" lands as filler, and everyone can feel it's filler. But *"you're in Melbourne - is it as brutally cold as it looks?"* lands as *attention*. The gap between the two isn't charm. It's simply whether you bothered to look.

So if the first two minutes have always felt like a performance you keep failing, stop performing. You were never meant to be entertaining. Notice one real thing about them. Offer one real thing back. Do that, and the warmth isn't something you have to fake your way toward - it just turns up on its own, because two people genuinely saw each other for a second.

That's connection. It was never charm. It was only ever noticing.
