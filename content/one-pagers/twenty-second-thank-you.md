# The 20-second thank-you

I've got a confession: until about a month ago, I was bulk-sending "thanks for connecting" messages myself.

Me. The bloke writing you a series about doing this properly. Same message, every new connection, fired off in batches when I got around to it. And I knew better - I just did it anyway, and I'd bet most of the people nagging you about personalisation do too.

Here's why everyone does it. When the connections are coming in - and if you've chosen your searches well, they come in fast - writing a genuine, personal message to each one means opening their profile, reading it properly, finding the thing worth saying, and writing it in your own words. Call it ten thoughtful minutes each. Ten minutes, times every new connection, forever. Nobody has that. So we all made the same quiet trade: send the same line to everyone, or send nothing at all.

And everyone on the receiving end can smell it. "Thanks for connecting! I'd love to learn more about your business" - you delete those. So do I. So does the person you just sent one to. The bulk thank-you isn't a small failure of effort; it actively spends the one moment of mutual attention you'll ever be handed for free. They just said yes to you. For about a day, you are allowed to be in their inbox. And the standard move is to walk into that moment holding a photocopy.

Here's the thing though - the trade we all made was forced on us by a constraint that no longer exists. **The habit is a relic. Personalisation used to be expensive; now it isn't.** The economics flipped: what took ten thoughtful minutes now takes about twenty seconds - long enough to glance at what was drafted from their profile, check it sounds like you, and press send. Handcrafted-at-scale stopped being a contradiction.

Twenty seconds only pays, mind you, because of the two decisions that came before it. If you connected with whoever the algorithm served up, a personal thank-you is lipstick on a cold list. But if you chose the room and searched for people positioned to trust and recommend you, then this message - built off *their* profile, about *their* work, with no pitch anywhere in it - lands on exactly the kind of person who notices the difference. That's the quiet one-two: selection makes personalisation worth doing; personalisation proves the selection was real. Do both and something remarkable happens: a reply, a real exchange, and more often than you'd believe, a call on the calendar within the fortnight.

What does a good one actually look like? Short. Specific - one real detail from their world, interpreted, not parroted back. Curious - it asks about them, because the whole approach is "let me find out about what you do." And patient - no pitch, no link, no "grab a time here." You're starting a conversation, not processing a lead.

So this week: when the next connection lands, don't reach for the photocopy. Open their profile, find the one thing worth being genuinely curious about, and spend the twenty seconds. Every relationship in this whole process starts inside that little window of mutual attention - it's the cheapest moment of leverage you'll get all week.

And when the conversation warms up and it's time to suggest a call? There's a right way and a wrong way to do that too - and the wrong way is the one everybody uses. Next time: why I'll never send you a Calendly link.
