Overcoming feast or famine

Why the busy-then-broke see-saw isn't a discipline problem - and what the businesses that escape it do differently.

Nobody warns you about the see-saw.

When you first go out on your own, the famine arrives before you've even printed business cards. So you work your network hard - coffees, calls, favours - and it works. You land those first clients. And then you do the thing every decent professional does: you throw yourself into serving them brilliantly. That's the responsible choice. That's who you are.

Network building quietly stops.

You feel it, too - that low-grade guilt about the catch-ups you're not having. But the client work is real and the networking is... postponable. Until the project wraps up, or the client's budget changes, or their new CFO has a mate in your line of work. Suddenly you're back where you started - except now the relationships you'd need are six months colder.

Here's the part nobody says out loud: it never goes away on its own. I've watched this cycle run in year one and in year twenty. However mature the business, the question underneath is identical - where is the next piece of revenue coming from?

And there are only two honest answers. You can advertise - rent a stranger's attention, at prices that climb every year, with an ROI you can never quite pin down. Or you can be known, remembered, and recommended - by people who already trust you. One of these compounds. The other one you buy again every month.

Most people know this. So why does the famine keep coming back?

Because of a quiet assumption almost everyone carries: that network building is an activity - something you do when you have time. And activities compete for hours. Put "coffee with an old colleague" up against "deadline for a paying client" and the coffee loses every single time, as it probably should. The see-saw isn't a discipline problem. It's built into the paradigm. You can't fix it by trying harder, because trying harder is exactly what you were doing when it broke.

The businesses that escape it make one mental shift: they stop treating their network as an activity and start treating it as infrastructure. Nobody does their bookkeeping "when things slow down." It runs every month, busy or not, because the consequences of letting it lapse are unacceptable. Your network is the same kind of asset - it just rarely gets treated like one.

prospect ending

So here's the question worth sitting with: if your network heard from you just as reliably in your busiest month as in your quietest one - what would that make possible? What work would you say yes to? What would you never again have to accept out of desperation?

Maybe you're already pushing back: you've been nagged about networking before, and it never seemed worth the hours it demanded. Fair - under the old way of doing it, it usually wasn't. That's the part we've quietly changed. Treating your network as infrastructure used to mean cloning yourself; now the right process and the right tools carry almost all of the load, and it fits inside a normal working week. That's the possibility this series is about - more on how next time.

Does it really work?

The introduction I made before we'd even spoken - a true story from a fortnight ago, with a timeline unheard of under the old rules of networking.

A fortnight ago I introduced two people to each other. Nothing unusual about that - except that one of them, I'd never actually spoken to myself.

Let me walk you through it, because the timeline is the whole point. I've changed the names; everything else is exactly as it happened.

I'd reached out to Andrew on LinkedIn the same way I reach out to anyone - a short note, written off his profile, about the work he actually does. No pitch, no angle. Just: you look like someone worth knowing, let me find out about what you do.

We got messaging back and forth, and the conversation drifted somewhere I've seen it drift before with fractional executives: how much they lean on each other. When you work across half a dozen businesses at once, you can't be across everything - so fractionals end up trusting other fractionals, and passing work between themselves, more than almost any group I know.

And then Andrew mentioned, almost in passing, that the place he was working with right now had a gap. They needed exactly the kind of help that a bloke in my network provides - Rob, a fractional CFO.

Now here's the part I want you to sit with. At this point, Andrew and I had never spoken. Not a phone call, not a Zoom, nothing - just messages. The old rules say this is far too early. You haven't earned it yet. Referrals are the payoff at the end of years of coffees and favours, the interest on trust you've slowly banked.

I introduced Rob anyway - before my own first call with Andrew had even happened.

The next day, Andrew came back to me: great call, perfect fit, he'd be recommending Rob to the business.

So what actually happened there? Not charm. Not history - there wasn't any. What made that introduction land was everything that happened before it: Andrew was in my world because I'd chosen him, deliberately. There's a first cut everyone does, and it matters - the right industry, enough years of experience, whether they've actually posted on LinkedIn lately or gone quiet. That tells you who's worth a look. But the filter that does the real work comes after it, and almost nobody applies it: attitude. Does this person think long-term about relationships? Do they share what they know? Do they talk about other people as generously as they talk about themselves? Andrew read that way from his profile onwards - and Rob was in my world for exactly the same reasons. When the need appeared, the introduction was almost mechanical - right person, meet right person. Two days, start to finish.

That's the thing nobody tells you about a well-chosen network: the introductions aren't a distant reward for years of relationship maintenance. They're just what happens when the right people are connected and actually talking. The years-of-coffees version isn't wrong because the trust is fake - it's wrong about where the trust comes from. It comes from who you selected - especially on attitude - far more than from how long you've known them.

And notice the direction it flowed. I didn't get a recommendation - I gave one, to someone in my network, from someone brand new to it. Which raises a question worth turning over: if introductions like that are moving through a network within days of someone joining it - what happens when you're the one standing in the middle of it?

That's where this series is heading. Next: why the way most of us were taught to network makes stories like this one basically impossible.

Pleasing methods, pleasing results

The breakfast clubs, the quota referrals, the eight-coffees-and-nothing day. They feel like networking. Here's why they don't build a network.

There's a kind of networking that feels wonderful and produces almost nothing. You know the one. You may well have paid for it.

The weekly breakfast. The badge, the round-table, the sixty-second pitch you've polished to a shine. A room of friendly faces, a coffee, a genuine warmth to it. You leave feeling like you did the thing - like a good, diligent professional who's out there, building. And that feeling is most of the problem, because the feeling is a lot of what you're paying for.

Let me be careful here. I'm not sneering at any of it. I ran networking meetings myself for years - in person and online - and did enormous amounts of matchmaking by hand. I know exactly why people love these rooms. The company is real. The mornings are pleasant. But I stopped, and I won't go back, and it's worth saying plainly why.

Start with the room itself. Who's in it? Whoever paid the membership. That's the whole selection - not the people best placed to send you work, not the ones whose judgement you'd stake your name on. Just whoever bought a seat, in whatever jumble of trades the morning happened to serve up. A mortgage broker, a printer, a life coach, and you.

Then there's the machinery. A lot of these groups run on a quota - you're expected to produce so many referrals a month. Think about what that does. It pushes you to recommend people - into an unfiltered room you didn't choose - not because you've found the right person for a real need, but because it's the end of the month and your number's due. Referrals become homework. And everyone can feel the difference between an introduction that was earned and one that was owed.

For a while it works well enough. The first year has a honeymoon - fresh faces, a few leads, the novelty of it. Then you've met everyone. The same people, the same pitches, the referrals thinning to a trickle of obligation. By year two a quiet weariness sets in, and you can't quite say why the thing that felt so productive stopped producing.

Here's the sharpest version of it. I once watched someone do eight one-on-ones in a single day at an event. Eight. They were shattered by the evening, and glowing - what a productive day. And nothing came of a single one. Not because the conversations were bad, but because there was no reason for any of them to go anywhere next, and not one of the eight had been chosen for anything beyond being in the building that day. Maximum effort. Maximum pleasantness. Zero mechanism for a result.

That's the trap in a word: these are pleasing methods. They please you - they feel like networking, they tick the box, they let you tell yourself you're doing the work. What they don't do is build a network.

And notice what I'm not saying. I'm not saying community is worthless, or that introductions over coffee are a bad idea - they're the whole point. The thing that's broken isn't the connection. It's the grind wrapped around it: the turning up, the chasing, the quota, the room you had no hand in choosing. Separate the grind from the gift. Keep the introduction. Shed the labour and the randomness that were never really helping.

Which points at the one thing every pleasing method has in common, sitting underneath all of it: you never got to choose who was in the room.

But what if you could? What if the room were yours to build, deliberately, before you shook a single hand?

That's next.

Choose, Don't Collect

This isn't bulk networking. You're choosing people through real conversation - almost interviewing them - for how they think and whether they'll build their own network too.

opening is verbatim the homepage "Start here" - add one echo-own line

Think about the last networking event you went to. Who was in the room?

Whoever paid to be there. That's the whole answer. Not the people best placed to send you work, not the people whose judgement you'd trust with your reputation - just whoever bought a ticket, in whatever mix of industries and intentions the morning happened to serve up. And then you did the polite, diligent thing: you worked with what the room gave you. Swapped cards with the mortgage broker. Had the coffee with the printer. Hoped.

We treat that as normal. It's worth saying out loud how strange it is.

In every other part of your business, you select. You don't hire whoever walks past the office. You don't take on every client who rings - you've learned the hard way what a wrong-fit client costs. But when it comes to the network - the asset that decides where your next years of revenue come from - most professionals accept whatever assortment of people circumstance deals them, and then wonder why the recommendations feel random.

Here's the shift: you're allowed to choose the room.

Not tweak it. Choose it. Decide, before you meet a single person, who actually belongs in your network - and go find those people, instead of hoping they find the same breakfast you did.

That flips the question from "how do I work this room?" to "who should be in the room at all?" And that's an upstream decision. It happens before the coffee, before the handshake, before the follow-up. Get it right and everything downstream gets easier, because you're building relationships with people you picked on purpose. Get it wrong - or never make it at all - and no amount of charm or follow-up discipline fixes it. You can't relationship your way out of a badly chosen room.

So what does choosing look like in practice? Two filters.

The first is position. LinkedIn means you can search the whole professional landscape and pull out exactly the kind of people you want - by role, by industry, by where they sit relative to your future clients. The person who belongs in your room usually isn't a buyer. It's someone embedded where your future clients live, who'll come to know your work well enough to put their name behind it.

The second filter is the one nobody automates: attitude. Some people get relationships and think in introductions; some collect contacts and think in transactions. You've met both. Only one of them belongs in a network you're going to invest years in - and you can tell which is which surprisingly early if you're actually looking for it.

Position, then attitude. Filter twice, and the room stops being an accident.

And notice what that means: the quality of your network was never really about how good you are at networking. The charm, the follow-up, the staying in touch - all of it multiplies whatever the room contains. Selection decides what there is to multiply.

prospect ending

So here's the question to sit with: if you could put twenty people in a room - any twenty, chosen deliberately for where they sit and how they think - who would they be? Not who do you know. Who would you choose?

Hold that picture - because that room isn't a fantasy. Imagine every one of those twenty coming to know your work well enough to put their name behind it, sending the right people your way without being asked. That's not a better networking event. It's a network you designed on purpose - and it can be built.

A thanks-for-connecting that delights

The 'thanks for connecting' everyone sends is a photocopy. The one that works reads their profile and genuinely delights them - and now it takes about twenty seconds.

I've got a confession: until about a month ago, I was bulk-sending "thanks for connecting" messages myself.

Me. The bloke writing you a series about doing this properly. And I wasn't being lazy - I was being rational. I had a paradigm fixed in my head: doing it properly, a genuine message written for each person, would simply take too long. So the standard "thanks for connecting" wasn't a lapse - given that belief, it was the only sensible option I had. I suspect that's exactly where most people sit: not careless, just quietly certain the good version can't be done at any real scale. Including, I'd bet, most of the people who nag you about personalisation.

Here's why everyone does it. When the connections are coming in - and if you've chosen and scored well, they come in fast - writing a genuine, personal message to each one means opening their profile, reading it properly, finding the thing worth saying, and writing it in your own words. Call it ten thoughtful minutes each. Ten minutes, times every new connection, forever. Nobody has that. So we all made the same quiet trade: send the same line to everyone, or send nothing at all.

And everyone on the receiving end can smell it. "Thanks for connecting! I'd love to learn more about your business" - you delete those. So do I. So does the person you just sent one to. The bulk thank-you isn't a small failure of effort; it actively spends the one moment of mutual attention you'll ever be handed for free. They just said yes to you. For about a day, you're allowed to be in their inbox. And the standard move is to walk into that moment holding a photocopy.

Now, one relief before we fix it: you don't have to hand-write to everyone. The system can send a simple, decent thanks automatically, so nobody you've connected with is ever left hanging. That's the floor. But the floor isn't the goal - and for the people worth it, you can do something in a completely different league.

Here's what everyone gets wrong about "personalisation." To most people - and most tools - it means dropping the person's first name into a template. "Hi Sarah, thanks for connecting!" That isn't personalisation. It's a mail-merge wearing a smile, and everyone sees straight through it.

What actually lands is different in kind, not degree. You read their profile - properly - and come back with something genuinely insightful about their work: a specific observation, a real question about the thing they clearly care about. The effect on the other end isn't "oh, they used my name" - it's "this person actually looked at me." People are rarely truly seen, and they don't forget the ones who bother. That's the whole distance between a message that gets deleted and a message that delights.

Then something I still half-think of as a small miracle: the constraint my whole habit was built on just dissolved. The system does the ten-minute part now. The AI reads the profile properly - the real substance of it - and drafts something genuinely insightful, in my voice. What's left for me is the twenty seconds that actually need a human: a glance to check it rings true, a small tweak if it doesn't, and send. The thing my paradigm swore was impossible turned out to be not just feasible but a genuine pleasure - I get to send something delightful to everyone worth it, and it costs me almost nothing.

Twenty seconds only pays, mind you, because of the two decisions that came before it. If you connected with whoever the algorithm served up, a delightful thank-you is lipstick on a cold list. But if you chose the room and scored for attitude, then this message - built off their profile, about their work, with no pitch anywhere in it - lands on exactly the kind of person who notices the difference. Selection makes the effort worth it; the effort proves the selection was real. Do both and something quietly remarkable happens: a real reply, a genuine exchange, and - often inside the fortnight - a "sure, worth a quick chat?" that you barely had to ask for.

What does a good one look like? Short. Specific - one real detail from their world, interpreted, never parroted back. Curious - it asks about them, because the whole approach is "let me find out about what you do." And patient - no pitch, no link, no "grab a time here." You're opening a conversation, not processing a lead.

prospect ending

Picture that at scale: every connection worth the effort getting a message that makes them feel genuinely seen - not ten minutes each, but twenty seconds - and the replies that come back when someone realises you actually looked. That's not a tidier inbox. It's the difference between a pile of contacts and the start of real conversations.

And when the conversation warms up and it's time to suggest a call? There's a right way and a wrong way to do that too - and the wrong way is the one everybody uses. Next time: why I'll rarely send you a Calendly link.

Why I'll rarely send you a Calendly link

A scheduling link feels like a courtesy. Look closer at what it quietly tells the person you're trying to win over.

I will rarely send you a Calendly link.

Not because I'm a technophobe - I've built my whole working life on good tools, and you'll hear me sing their praises before this series is done. When I do hold off, it's because of what it quietly says to you. And once you see it, you won't be able to unsee it either.

Picture the moment. You've reached out to someone worth knowing, sent them a genuine note, had a real exchange, and they've come back with the thing you were hoping for: "Yeah, I'd be happy to have a chat." You've won the hard part. And this is precisely where most people fumble it - they fire back a link. "Great! Grab a time that works for you: calendly.com/…"

It feels like the considerate, modern move. I'm saving us both the back-and-forth. But look at what it actually does.

A scheduling link says two things, and neither of them is what you want. The first: my time is the fixed point, and you fit yourself around it. You've just handed a person you're trying to impress a small chore - open the link, scan a grid, hold their week up against your availability, pick, confirm. The mental load you didn't fancy? You've quietly posted it to them.

The second is worse. It says: you're a slot in my system. Everything you've done so far - chosen this person deliberately, thanked them like they mattered - and then, at the first practical step, you process them like a support ticket. The medium undoes the message.

Here's what I do instead, and it takes about thirty seconds. "Would Tuesday afternoon or Thursday morning suit you? Happy to shift around whatever works." Two or three considered times, offered the way one person offers another. It says the exact opposite of the link: I looked at my week, I thought about yours, and you're worth the small effort of doing this properly.

Now, I can hear the objection, because it's a good one. "That's the entire reason Calendly exists! The back-and-forth is a nightmare - three emails just to land on a time." And you're right. It used to be. That pain was real, and the link did solve it - by shoving the pain onto the other person.

But here's the shift: the back-and-forth isn't yours to suffer either, not anymore. The system reads the thread, sees you've agreed to talk, offers times that genuinely fit your calendar, and the moment they pick one, books it and sends the confirmation with the link. The considerate version and the effortless version used to be opposites. They simply aren't now.

If that sounds familiar, it should - it's the same shift as the thank-you. Just as a genuine, personal note became cheap enough to send every single time, considered scheduling became cheap enough to offer every single time. Effort that reads as care is no longer expensive. Which removes the last excuse for outsourcing that effort to the very person you're trying to win over.

prospect ending

Rarely isn't never - a big group to herd, someone who genuinely prefers a link. But think about the last time you were made to feel like a slot in someone's system. Now imagine a network built the other way: every first step telling people they're a person to you, not a calendar event - and costing you almost nothing to do. That's the small hinge a whole relationship swings on.

And then they turn up to the call. Which is where the biggest paradigm of the lot is waiting - because almost everyone walks into that first conversation and does the one thing guaranteed to sink it. Next time: what your first discovery call is actually for.

A reason to follow up

Great conversations don't die from a lack of goodwill. They die from a lack of a next thing. Here's how to make sure there's always a reason to come back.

You've had one of those coffees. Ninety good minutes, real rapport, the conversation ranging easily across what you both do. You stand up saying - and meaning - "we should definitely stay in touch," maybe even "let's keep an eye out for each other." You leave a little lifted.

And then nothing happens.

Not out of rudeness. A week goes by, then a month. The warmth quietly cools. Six months on you'd struggle to remember what you talked about, and the introduction you were each going to make never materialised. Two people who genuinely got on, drifting apart for no dramatic reason at all.

Here's why it happens, and it's almost never a lack of goodwill. We're taught to believe relationships are held together by how much we like each other - by good intentions and warm feeling. So we lean on "we got on well, we'll stay in touch," and trust the fondness to do the work.

It won't. Goodwill doesn't sustain a relationship. Motion does. What keeps two people connected isn't the warmth of the first meeting - it's having a reason to come back. Something to talk about next. Take that away and even the best conversation is a lovely dead end.

So the real question after any good meeting isn't "did we click?" It's "what's the next thing?"

And the answer is almost never "so - got any referrals for me?" That's the transactional move, and people can smell it a mile off; it's the surest way to guarantee there is no next time. The reasons that actually pull a relationship forward run the other way. Something you came across that would genuinely help them. A person in your world they ought to meet. A thought you've been turning over since they said something that stuck. You give a reason to reconnect; you don't ask for one.

If you followed the last piece, you've already got the cleanest version of this handed to you. Keep that first call short and all about them, and you walk away holding the reason on a plate: "you mentioned you were wrestling with X - I've been thinking about it, and I know exactly who you should talk to." No manufacturing required.

That give-and-introduce rhythm alone will keep most relationships alive for years - over time you can nearly always think of someone worth introducing them to, and those introductions are reason enough to stay close. That works. But there's a version that works far better, and it turns an occasional reason to follow up into a permanent one.

Every so often, as you get to know someone, you'll realise they're a builder - the kind of person who lights up at the idea of a network, not just a contact list, and who'd happily grow one of their own if they knew how. When you meet one, don't just keep in touch. Invite them into exactly what you're doing: show them how you go about it, and quietly agree to build each other's networks.

Look at what that changes. The moment the two of you are both out there, deliberately, looking out for one another, the reason to follow up never runs dry again. Every good person they meet is a possible introduction for you; every one you meet is one for them. Getting to know new people, chasing down leads, making introductions - it stops being effort you have to motivate and becomes the natural business of two people building something together. Help them build their network, and their network quietly becomes part of yours.

So don't hand your best conversations over to goodwill and hope. Before you leave one, plant the reason to return - something to share, someone to introduce, a thread to pick up next time. Relationships live on motion.

Keep that up, with the right people, and a curious thing happens. Somewhere along the way you cross a quiet line - from someone who networks to someone who builds - and what began as tending a handful of relationships one at a time turns into something that grows on its own. That's the whole point of everything so far, and it's where we finish.

What can you discover?

The first call fills people with dread - because they think it's a pitch they can fail. It isn't. It's the easiest conversation you'll ever have, once you know what you're really there to find out.

Most people walk into their first call with a knot in their stomach.

They've rehearsed how to explain what they do. They're braced for the awkward silence, the polite "hmm, not quite what we're after," the small rejection they can feel coming. Because somewhere along the way they decided this call is a performance - a pitch, a test where they're the one being marked. And nobody enjoys a test they might fail.

Here's the thing: that dread is entirely manufactured. It comes from one wrong idea about what the call is for.

You think the discovery call is where you present yourself - make a good enough case that this person will want what you offer. So you prepare your spiel, you polish your explanation, and you steel yourself for the verdict. The whole encounter is set up, in your head, as something you can pass or fail.

Flip it. The call isn't a pitch. It's a discovery. You're not there to be assessed - you're there to learn. And the moment that lands, the fear simply drains out of it, because nobody has ever been rejected for taking a genuine interest in another human being.

So - what can you discover?

What they actually do, for one - not the LinkedIn headline, the real texture of their week. How they think, up close: the attitude you selected for, now in the flesh. What they're building, what's hard about it, what they're quietly proud of. And the big one - who they know. Because here is the reframe that changes the entire character of the call: this person does not matter because they might buy from you. They matter because of who they might one day recommend you to.

And it runs both ways. As you listen, you're also discovering who you could send their way - the introduction you could make, the person in your world they'd love to meet. A good discovery call isn't extraction; it's the first exchange in something mutual. You each leave it thinking about who the other should meet.

Notice what that does to the pressure. When there is nothing to sell, there is nothing to be rejected about. The call that had you rehearsing in the car becomes the easiest conversation in the world - you're simply a curious person, genuinely interested, finding out about someone worth knowing. That's not a technique. It's just what's left when you stop trying to close.

If you've fumbled a few of these already - walked in and started pitching, felt it curdle - don't wince. That's the old paradigm doing exactly what it does. Most people only work this out the hard way, mid-call, wishing they'd started differently. You get to know it before you sit down.

prospect ending

You might be wondering what you actually say for half an hour when you're not pitching - and yes, there's a craft to that. But set it aside for now. Just sit with the shift itself: the call that had you rehearsing in the car becomes the easiest conversation in the world. Picture a whole run of them ahead - not pitches to survive, but people worth knowing, each one leaving with you in mind for the next introduction they make. That's what a first call becomes the moment you stop trying to sell and start trying to discover.

Connection isn't charm

The first two minutes of a call fill technical people with dread. Here's the secret nobody tells you: connection isn't charm, and it isn't small talk - it's a procedure anyone can run.

now owns the homepage’s "not a natural networker" gateway

For a lot of people - and technical people most of all - the scariest part of meeting someone new isn't the substance. It's the first two minutes.

The bit before you get down to it - a call, a coffee, a first conversation of any kind. The "how's your weekend, how's the weather" bit. If you're someone who'd rather just talk about the actual work, this stretch can feel like a tax you don't know how to pay - a performance of easy chit-chat that some people seem born for and you, apparently, are not. So you rush it, or skip it, and the thing never quite warms up.

Here's the secret nobody tells you: connection isn't charm. It isn't banter, it isn't quick wit, and it is absolutely not a personality you're either born with or you're not. It's a procedure - two small moves - and anyone can run it, including you.

Here they are.

Move one: notice one true, specific thing about them - and say it.

Not "nice weather." Specific. Something actually in front of you. The city they're in. The bookshelf or the plants behind them. A post they wrote. Where they used to work. You're not manufacturing a compliment - you're noticing something real and mentioning it out loud. "I was just reading your post from a few months back - you clearly threw everything at that." "I love the plants behind you - that's not a virtual background, is it?" "You're in Sydney - that's where I started out."

That's the whole move. And the other person's reaction isn't "what a charmer." It's the quiet, disarming feeling of being seen - and people relax the instant they feel seen. It's the same thing a good thank-you message does, just out loud: specific attention, freely given.

Move two: offer one true, specific thing back.

Connection is a trade, not an interview. So don't just ask "how was your weekend?" and nod along - answer it yourself, with something real. "Mine was good - I've just taken up pickleball, no idea what I'm doing." "Sorry, I'm a bit scattered - the power just went out at my daughter's place, they're putting in a pool out the back." A small, human, slightly imperfect detail of your own. It says: I'm a person here too, not a script. And it quietly gives them permission to be one back.
